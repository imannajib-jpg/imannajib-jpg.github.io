About

I started building at 15. I founded FIRST Robotics Team 5130 at a school with no computer science or STEM teachers, taught myself to program from public forums and documentation, and made the case to the local school board for real investment in STEM, securing $10,000 in funding for the program.

Since then I have led the team to the national championship and still mentor it today. I care most about systems I can own end to end, where the mechanical, electrical, and software pieces all have to work together under real conditions, not just on a bench.

I study Computer Science and Political Science at Williams College.

My most recent robot, built to push the vision side of autonomy. It carries the same swerve and CAN bus architecture as my competition robot, but the emphasis here is on perception: getting a camera to tell the robot precisely where it is and what it is looking at, then acting on that. Modeled in Fusion 360.

// vision & perception
  • Limelight smart camera used as a dedicated vision coprocessor.
  • Tuned against the official Limelight documentation, with AprilTag calibration for accurate tag detection and pose estimation.
  • Pose data feeds the control loop so the robot can locate and align to targets autonomously.
// shared architecture
  • Swerve-style omnidirectional drivetrain.
  • Motor controllers networked over a daisy-chained CAN bus.
  • Programmed in Java.
// overview

The swerve-drive competition robot I founded the team to design and build, programmed in Java on WPILib and modeled in Fusion 360. Omnidirectional drivetrain on an aluminum frame, with a full sensor and vision stack and scripted autonomous routines.

// drivetrain & motion
  • Swerve modules for omnidirectional movement on an aluminum chassis.
  • Spark MAX motor controllers driving the drivetrain, tuned with PID for precise speed and heading.
  • CTRE Pigeon 2 IMU for field-relative orientation and balancing routines.
  • Dedicated climber subsystem geared at a higher reduction ratio for the extra torque and holding power needed to lift the robot.
// electrical & power
  • 12V battery feeding a Power Distribution Panel through correctly gauged wiring.
  • Motor controllers daisy-chained on a CAN bus network for communication.
// control & vision
  • RoboRIO as the main robot controller.
  • USB web camera streamed for driver view.
  • Limelight coprocessor with onboard camera for AprilTag detection.
// autonomy & software
  • Written in Java on the WPILib framework.
  • PathPlanner used to author field routines and full autonomous paths.
// fabrication
  • Modeled in Fusion 360.
  • Structural panels were power-cut: started in acrylic, then moved to polycarbonate to eliminate stress fractures and survive vibration and impact.
